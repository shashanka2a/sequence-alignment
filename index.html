What is Sequence Alignment?

Sequence alignment is the process of arranging DNA, RNA, or protein sequences to identify regions of similarity that may indicate functional, structural, or evolutionary relationships.

Learn More

Core Concepts

Global alignment, implemented using the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm, compares sequences from start to end, ensuring alignment across their full length.

Local alignment, implemented using the Smith-Waterman algorithm, finds the most similar regions within sequences, making it useful for identifying conserved domains.

Dovetail alignment aligns sequences by allowing gaps at the ends without penalties, useful in scenarios like genome assembly where overlapping regions are aligned.

Real-World Applications

Sequence alignment is a powerful technique used across various fields in bioinformatics and life sciences. Here are some key applications with detailed explanations and examples.

Sequence alignment helps in comparing genomes of different species to identify genetic variations, mutations, and conserved regions. This is crucial in studying evolution, diagnosing genetic disorders, and identifying disease-related genes.

  • Comparative genomics for evolutionary studies.
  • Identifying mutations related to diseases.
  • Genome-wide association studies (GWAS).

By aligning sequences from different organisms, scientists can construct phylogenetic trees to trace evolutionary relationships. This helps in understanding how species have evolved and identifying common ancestors.

  • Building phylogenetic trees.
  • Tracing the origins of viruses and bacteria.
  • Studying evolutionary divergence.

Sequence alignment is used to compare protein sequences to identify functional domains and predict protein structures. This is essential for drug discovery, understanding diseases, and annotating new proteins.

  • Identifying conserved protein domains.
  • Predicting protein structure and function.
  • Targeting proteins for drug development.
